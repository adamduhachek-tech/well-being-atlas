After George Floyd: the reckoning that snapped back

When George Floyd was killed on May 25, 2020, American opinion on the police and on race lurched in a single direction within days. A year later, most of that movement was gone.

Data Nationscape, weekly 2019–2021 Sample 494,532 respondents across 85 waves Method weighted to U.S. adult population targets

For most of a year before the spring of 2020, American attitudes toward the police barely moved. Week after week, in a survey that interviewed roughly six thousand adults every seven days, the share saying they felt favorably toward “the police” sat in the mid-seventies. In the four-and-a-half months before George Floyd was killed it averaged 76.1%, weighted to the U.S. adult population. It was one of the steadier lines in American public opinion.

Then a Minneapolis police officer knelt on George Floyd’s neck for more than nine minutes, the video circled the world, and the line broke. By June, favorability toward the police had fallen to 61.3% — a drop of 14.8 percentage points in a matter of weeks, the steepest move in the entire series. The week Floyd died, the line was already cracking, at 64.8%; by the trough of 59.4% in mid-June it had lost a sixth of its level. At the same time, the share of Americans who said Black people face “a great deal” or “a lot” of discrimination jumped 8.9 points, belief in structural racism rose 5.6, and racial resentment fell 3.9. Every needle on the dashboard swung toward the protesters at once.

That much is the story almost everyone remembers: the summer of 2020 as a genuine, measurable shift in how the country talked about race and policing. The part that gets told less often is what happened next. The reckoning did not hold. By the first months of 2021, police favorability had climbed back to 72.6% — meaning only 23.6% of the June drop was still there. Perceived anti-Black discrimination had given back almost everything: just 5.6% of its jump survived. The country moved, hard and fast and in unison — and then, just as clearly, moved most of the way back.

The cliff, and the climb back

The clearest way to see both halves of the story is to watch a single attitude week by week across the whole window and mark the day Floyd died. Use the controls to switch the measure, and to split the country into its political parties or its racial groups — the cliff appears in every line, but it is far from the same size in each.

▲
Watching the By party view of police favorability is the fastest way to feel the asymmetry. Democrats fell off a cliff — −22.0 points by June — while Republicans, already near the ceiling, barely flinched at −6.5. By 2021 both had recovered most of the ground.

Who moved the most

A national average flattens a very uneven shock. The collapse in police favorability was concentrated among the groups already most skeptical — and it was those same groups whose numbers had climbed back the least a year later. Democrats and Black Americans lived the sharpest version of both the drop and, for everyone else, the rebound.

Among Democrats, favorability toward the police fell from 70.1% before Floyd to 48.1% in June, a 22.0-point plunge, before recovering to 64.8%. Among Black respondents the drop was nearly as steep, 21.8 points — from 55.5% to a remarkable June low of 33.7% — and their recovery was the most incomplete of any racial group, with only 7.8% of the move retrieved by 2021. Republicans, starting at 87.5%, had the least room and the smallest fall.

What moved, and what snapped back

Police favorability and perceived discrimination were not the only attitudes to twitch in June 2020 — but they moved the most, and they also reverted the most. Set every measure side by side and a pattern emerges: the two attitudes most directly about policing and anti-Black discrimination show the largest June shifts and, by 2021, the smallest residue. The slower-moving beliefs — whether Americans feel warmly toward Black people, and whether they endorse a racial-resentment talking point — barely budged in June and, where they did, held a larger fraction of that small move.

Belief that “generations of slavery and discrimination” hold Black Americans back rose 5.6 points to a June peak, then surrendered most of it: only 12.5% remained by 2021. Racial resentment — the view that Black people should advance “without special favors” like other minorities — fell 3.9 points in June and had fully returned, and then some, a year on. The reckoning showed up across the board; it lasted nowhere.

What this shows — and what it does not

This is a description of what a large, repeatedly-sampled cross-section of Americans told an interviewer, week by week, weighted to look like the adult population. It is not an experiment. The survey cannot prove that Floyd’s killing caused the swing, only that opinion moved sharply in its immediate aftermath and along the lines the protests were drawing; the timing is tight and the move is enormous, but a parade of other 2020 events — the pandemic, lockdowns, a bitter election — ran alongside it. Each weekly point is an independent sample of different people, not the same respondents tracked over time, so these are shifts in the national mix of opinion, not documented changes of heart inside individual minds.

The recovery is the most robust finding here, and it squares with what other researchers using Nationscape and the Democracy Fund’s Voter Study Group reported as the “racial reckoning” cooled: a real spike in support for Black Lives Matter and in concern about discrimination over the summer of 2020, followed by a steady fade back toward where opinion had started, with some groups settling slightly off their old baselines. Subgroup retention figures for the smaller racial categories swing wildly — when a June shift is tiny, the share “retained” can balloon past 100% or flip negative on ordinary sampling noise — so this piece leans on the groups with the largest, cleanest moves and treats the rest as texture. The headline is not that nothing changed. It is that the country changed a great deal, very fast, and then most of it changed back.

Notes & data

  • Source. Nationscape, a project of UCLA and the Democracy Fund + UCLA Nationscape / Democracy Fund Voter Study Group. 85 weekly waves, July 15, 2019 to April 12, 2021; 494,532 respondents across the series, several thousand per week.
  • Weighting. Every estimate uses the survey’s weight variable, post-stratified to U.S. adult population targets. Figures are described as “weighted to U.S. adult population targets.” These are weighted point estimates; survey-design standard errors are not reported.
  • Event. George Floyd was killed on Monday, May 25, 2020. Weeks are labeled by their Monday start, so the wave beginning 2020-05-25 is the “Floyd week.”
  • Windows. Pre = Jan 1–May 18, 2020; June peak = June 1–30, 2020; 2021 = Jan 1–Apr 15, 2021. The June shift is the June mean minus the pre mean; retention is (2021 − pre) ÷ (June − pre).
  • Suppression. Weekly points with effective N below 100 are dropped (this removes a handful of degenerate end-of-series waves with one or two respondents). Subgroup-by-week cells below that threshold are likewise suppressed.
  • Measures. All five attitudes are 0/1 indicators whose weighted mean is a share: favorable toward the police; perceives “a great deal / a lot” of discrimination against Black people (and, for contrast, against White people); favorable toward Black people; agrees generations of slavery and discrimination make it harder for Black people to advance (structural-racism belief); agrees Black people should advance without special favors as other minorities did (racial resentment).
  • Subgroups. Party is self-reported three-way (Democrat / Independent / Republican); race is five-way (White / Black / Hispanic / Asian / Other). Party colors — Democratic ■, Republican ■, Independent grey — are used only where party is the subject; racial groups use a colorblind-safe categorical palette.
  • What it is. A weighted descriptive time series of repeated cross-sections — association and timing, not causation; shifts in the population mix of opinion, not within-person change.
  • Prior art. Consistent with reporting from the Nationscape / Democracy Fund Voter Study Group and from researchers (e.g., analyses of Black Lives Matter support in 2020) documenting a sharp summer-2020 shift in racial attitudes that substantially reverted afterward.
