A data essay · five charts, one argument

Never Enough

Why getting richer delivers so little — and what money actually buys.

We spend our lives chasing more, and the economy obliges: the average American family is about a quarter richer, in real terms, than it was in 1972. By rights we should be more satisfied with our money than our grandparents were. We are not. To see why, you have to stop asking how much people have and start asking where they stand.

Act one

The treadmill

Run the numbers and the promise fails to arrive. Adjusted for inflation, average family income rose about 25 percent from the early 1970s to today. Over the same half-century, the share of Americans "pretty well satisfied" with their finances did not rise to meet it — it slipped, from 32 percent to 28. The line that should have climbed with the money drifted the other way. Economists call it the satisfaction treadmill: you walk faster and stay in place.

So more money for the average American bought no more peace about money. That is strange enough to demand an explanation. If it isn't the dollars, what is it?

Act two

It was never the money. It was the standing.

Ask people not what they earn but where they think they sit — "far below average," "average," "above average" — and the fog lifts. Financial satisfaction climbs almost a full point up that ladder of relative position. And when you put dollars and rank in the same model, the dollars nearly vanish: adding relative position collapses the effect of real income by 60 percent. What money was buying, all along, was a place in line.

And the standing matters more than it used to. The financial-satisfaction gap between the richest and poorest quarter of Americans has widened — from 0.48 points in the 1970s to 0.70 today — even as the average held flat. The race didn't end. The track got steeper.

Act three

Your yardstick is your neighbors

Relative to whom? Not some abstract national mean — the people you can see. Take two Americans on the identical paycheck and drop one into a richer county. Holding income, age, education, race and year all fixed, the one surrounded by wealthier neighbors rates his life lower. The effect is modest but real: doubling a county's median income costs about seven-hundredths of a ladder point. And it bites the middle — not the poorest, for whom a richer county, with its services and spillovers, slightly helps.

Money satisfies relative to a line — and the line is drawn by the people next door.

Act four

A human universal — and what outranks it

Is this an American neurosis or a human one? Across 108 countries and 40 years of the World Values Survey, your income rank within your own country predicts your life satisfaction everywhere — a touch more strongly, if anything, in poorer places. But run the real contest — rank against the things money is supposed to deliver — and a different winner emerges. Once you account for how financially secure people feel and how much freedom they sense over their own lives, raw income rank all but disappears. Perceived freedom out-predicts it roughly seven to one.

Money's pull on a life runs through feeling — and feeling answers to more than money.

Act five

The honest ledger

So set the evidence side by side. A higher income rank lifts how people rate their lives the most. It does move the day, a little, in both directions — a bit more joy, a bit less worry — but the rating gains about half again as much, and even there, freedom rivals or beats it. The ledger reads plainly: money buys you a better position, a higher number when you stop to take stock, and a measure of calm. On its own it does not buy a better day.

The richest society in history is still on the treadmill, and now we can say why. The line we measure ourselves against — our neighbors, our era, our own rising expectations — keeps moving with us. And the things that would actually lift the day, a sense of freedom and someone to count on, were never for sale. We will always have more. It will never be enough, because enough was never a quantity. It was a place in line.

Notes & data

Method. Effects are standardized (per standard deviation). Cross-country models (World Values Survey) use country (and wave) fixed effects — each person compared within their own society — and the S017 design weight; never a population-weighted world average. GSS uses wtssps; the Gallup US Daily extract carries no survey weight, so those estimates are unweighted and describe patterns, not population levels. Evaluative measures (the ladder, life satisfaction, financial satisfaction) are kept distinct from experiential ones (yesterday's worry and joy). Effects here are real but modest — the honest size of well-being effects.

Prior art this builds on and departs from: Easterlin (1974) on growth and happiness; Kahneman & Deaton (2010) and Killingsworth-Kahneman-Mellers (2023) on income and the two measures; Luttmer (2005) on neighbors' earnings; Stevenson & Wolfers (2008). Every figure is computed by a reproducible script and traced to a per-claim provenance file.
